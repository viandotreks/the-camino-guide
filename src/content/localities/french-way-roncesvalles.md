Roncesvalles is less a town than a monastery with a village attached — a few dozen permanent residents, a collegiate church, and one of the largest pilgrim hostels on the entire Camino. It has been receiving pilgrims since the twelfth century, which means it knows how to do it.

The **Real Colegiata de Roncesvalles** was founded around 1132 by the Bishop of Pamplona to shelter pilgrims making the Pyrenean crossing. The church, the cloister, and the old chapter house survive in various states of preservation. The silver statue of the Virgin inside the church — *Nuestra Señora de Roncesvalles* — has been receiving offerings from pilgrims for nearly nine hundred years. Whether that continuity moves you or not, it is difficult to stand in front of it on your first evening and feel nothing.

The **Silo de Carlomagno**, a twelfth-century chapel that served as the first church on this site, is now a small museum. The ossuary beneath the collegiate church holds the bones of pilgrims and soldiers who died in the pass over the centuries. Access is limited.

The **Battle of Roncevaux Pass** (778 AD) was fought somewhere near here — or near Valcarlos, or near Altabiscar, depending on whose account you follow. Charlemagne's rearguard, retreating through the pass after a failed campaign in Spain, was ambushed. Roland died. The *Chanson de Roland* turned the ambush into an epic battle against Saracens; the historical record suggests it was Basque warriors protecting their territory. The landscape absorbed it either way.

For most pilgrims, Roncesvalles is a transition: the last night before Spain proper, the first hot meal in Spain, the first stamp in the *credencial*. Don't rush out in the morning. The collegiate church at dawn, when the fog is still on the beech forest, is worth getting up early for.
