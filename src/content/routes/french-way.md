The French Way is the oldest, most walked, and most documented route to Santiago. It begins in the French Basque Country, crosses the Pyrenees into Navarra, and follows an 800-year-old path through the meseta, Galicia, and into Santiago. Of the roughly 400,000 pilgrims who complete a Camino each year, the majority walk this one.

Its popularity is both its main quality and its main limitation. Infrastructure is excellent — albergues every few kilometers for most of the route, a well-marked path, emergency services, and a community of pilgrims dense enough that you're rarely alone in difficulty. But if you're looking for solitude, the French Way in June is not where you'll find it.

The route breaks into three broad sections. **Navarra and La Rioja** (stages 1–7) is the most dramatic: the Pyrenean crossing, the descent into Pamplona, the wine country of La Rioja. The **Meseta** (stages 10–17 approximately) is the part that frightens people in advance and surprises them once they're on it — a high plateau, wide and treeless, hot in summer, cold in spring, but with a particular quality of light and silence that many pilgrims name as the best part of the walk. **Galicia** (the final 100 km) is green, mountainous, and increasingly dense with pilgrims as you approach Santiago.

The traditional route as documented here follows the historical 33-stage division from Saint-Jean-Pied-de-Port. You can adjust — many pilgrims split or combine stages — but the traditional stages are the unit the infrastructure is built around.
